# Casual & Conversational Language — V10

V10 treats conversational language as a distinct learning skill rather than hiding it inside ordinary grammar cards.

## Why it is separate

A learner may know the fully explicit or polite form but still fail to understand what happens in real conversation when speakers:

- omit recoverable information
- contract common sequences
- change sentence endings
- choose a more spoken word
- rely on shared context

AIDA therefore stores **Casual register** as its own FSRS skill.

## Practice cycle

Each casual item rotates through three tasks.

### 1. Transform

You see a neutral, polite, or fully explicit sentence and try to produce a natural conversational version.

### 2. Notice

You compare the explicit and conversational forms and identify what changed:

- omitted particle or argument
- contraction
- sentence-final particle
- lexical replacement
- register shift

### 3. Register judgment

You decide where the form is natural and review the caution. This prevents learning a casual form as a universal replacement.

## Japanese

The bank currently contains **30 focused conversational items** from N5 through N1/N2-range spoken phenomena.

Major areas include:

- plain forms instead of `です / ます`
- casual yes/no questions without final `か`
- omission of `を`, `は`, `が`, and `に` when the missing relation is recoverable
- `ている → てる`
- `という → って`
- `では → じゃ`
- `てしまう → ちゃう / じゃう`
- `なくては → なくちゃ`
- `なければ → なきゃ`
- `ておく → とく`
- `ておいて → といて`
- `ては → ちゃ`
- `というか → っていうか`
- `もの → もん`
- shortened requests
- layered omission in conversation
- selected colloquial potential forms, with register cautions

### Particle omission rule

V10 does **not** teach “delete particles to sound casual.” Omission is presented only when the relationship is recoverable from the words, discourse, or situation. The reflection panel states what disappeared and warns against overgeneralization.

## Cantonese

The bank currently contains **30 focused conversational items** from Beginner through Advanced.

Major areas include:

- sentence-final particles such as `呀`, `啦`, `喎`, `咩`, `㗎`, `啫`, and `囉`
- particle combinations such as `㗎啦`, `呀嘛`, and `啫喎`
- subject and object omission when recoverable
- ongoing/result/state aspect with `緊`, `咗`, and `住`
- common spoken question forms such as `係咪`, `有冇`, `點解`, and `做乜`
- spoken forms such as `啲` and `咁`
- spoken-vs-written lexical replacement
- discourse continuation with `呢`

Jyutping is shown on reveal for every Cantonese casual item.

## Scheduling

Casual practice uses the same FSRS engine as the other skills, but the memory state is independent. A learner can therefore be strong at formal grammar while still having a due conversational review.
